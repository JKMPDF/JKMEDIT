Split a PDF into Pages or Custom Ranges

Splitting a PDF lets you extract individual pages or specific page ranges from a larger document. It is the right tool when you need to share a single chapter from a textbook, pull one signed page out of a 50-page contract, separate invoices into per-customer files, or break a large scanned document into smaller pieces for an upload portal. Pages are extracted exactly as they appear in the source — no re-rendering, no quality loss, and no watermark.

Three Ways You Can Split

  • Extract specific pages: Pick page 3, 7, and 12 from a long document and save them as a new PDF in one click.
  • Split by range: Save pages 1–10 as one file and 11–20 as another. Useful when sending different sections to different people.
  • Split every page into its own PDF: Turn a 50-page document into 50 separate single-page files. Common for batch printing or per-page archiving.

Real Situations Where Splitting Helps

HR teams routinely receive a single combined PDF containing resume, ID proof, and offer letter, and need to file each section separately. Lawyers extract individual exhibits from court bundles. Accountants split a year's worth of bank statements into per-quarter files for audit. Teachers share only the relevant chapter of a workbook with students. In all of these, splitting saves the recipient from scrolling through irrelevant pages and helps you stay organised on your own end too.

How the Splitter Preserves Quality

The tool uses pdf-lib to copy pages at the binary level. That means embedded fonts, vector graphics, hyperlinks, form fields, and bookmarks within the extracted range are carried over. The output is a fresh, valid PDF — not a screenshot. You can sign, search, and print the result the same way as the original. Compare this with screen-capturing pages, which produces an image-only PDF that loses searchable text and prints poorly.

Common Pitfalls to Avoid

If your source PDF is password-protected, you will need to remove the password first using a desktop viewer; encrypted PDFs cannot be re-saved by browser libraries. If the document was created from a scanner, splitting works but the resulting pages will still be image-only — run them through an OCR service afterwards if you need searchable text. And if you accidentally select the wrong range, just refresh the page and start again; nothing is committed until you click download.

Privacy: Splitting Stays on Your Device

Contracts, payslips, and medical reports are exactly the kind of files people split before forwarding. Uploading them to a random server is a privacy risk you do not need to take. This splitter does everything in your browser. Your file content does not leave the page. Once you close the tab, the parsed PDF is cleared from memory by your browser.

Frequently Asked Questions

How many pages can I split out at once?
There is no fixed cap. Most laptops handle splitting documents up to a few hundred pages and a few hundred megabytes without issue. Performance depends on your device's memory.

Will the page numbers, bookmarks, and links carry over?
Page content (text, fonts, images, links inside the kept range) is preserved. The original page number visible inside the page is preserved exactly. Bookmarks pointing to pages you keep are retained; bookmarks pointing to pages you removed are dropped.

Can I split a scanned PDF?
Yes. The tool treats scanned and digital PDFs the same way at the page level. The extracted pages remain as scans (images), not searchable text.

What if I want the opposite — to remove certain pages and keep the rest?
Split out the pages you want to keep, then either save those as your final file or merge them back together using our Merge PDF tool.

Do I get a single ZIP if I split into many files?
When you split every page into separate PDFs, the tool offers each download individually. For very long documents, splitting into a smaller number of ranges is usually more practical.
